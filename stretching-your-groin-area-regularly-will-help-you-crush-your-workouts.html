Stretching Your Groin Area Regularly Will Help You Crush Your Workouts

When I think about jumping or sprinting, I immediately assume my quads and glutes are powering my bod. I'm not wrong, but there's a key muscle group I'm missing. You may not think about it until it gets tight or achey, but the groin is a superstar when it comes to powering performance. Stay with me. The groin propels your body in multiple directions, all while keeping your torso strong and stable.

That means when you’re running, walking, bending forward, doing a cardio workout—literally moving in any direction—your groin is working overtime. But, too much sitting can cause it to tighten up quick, according to Annie Mulgrew, CPT, founding instructor for CITYROW in New York City and NASM-certified personal trainer.

Meet the experts: Annie Mulgrew, CPT, is the founding instructor for CITYROW in New York City and a NASM-certified personal trainer. Winnie Yu, DPT, CPT, is a sports and orthopedic physical therapist s at Bespoke Treatments in New York City.

The groin muscles play a critical role in movement and stability, so it’s super-important the area is both limber and strong, according to Mulgrew. These muscles can get into an overactive state if they’re compensating for weak gluteus medius muscles to stabilize the hip, according to Winnie Yu, DPT, CPT, a sports and orthopedic physical therapist s at Bespoke Treatments in New York City.

The groin is a pretty complex area, consisting of three fairly large muscle groups—the abdominals, iliopsoas, and adductors:

  • Your adductors work together to adduct (or move toward the midline of the body) the thigh while keeping your lower extremities and pelvis stabilized.
  • The abdominal muscles help stabilize your spine.
  • The iliopsoas, on either side of your hips, help stabilize and flex your hips and stabilize your lower back.

The muscles in the groin are often overlooked, which can lead to tightness, Yu adds. Make time for a few minutes of groin stretches to release that tension—and keep it away.

How To Stretch The Groin Area

You can separate stretching into two different categories: dynamic and static.

  • “Dynamic stretching is a great way to prepare for a workout,” Mulgrew says. (Try this five-minute dynamic stretching routine.) “If you’ve been immobile, or sitting, for an extended period of time, it’s best to stretch [the groin] in a dynamic way similar to a yoga flow, moving fluidly from stretch to stretch to increase the heat in the area and minimize stiffness.”
  • Static stretching, on the other hand, focuses on holding stretches and positions for a period of time, says Mulgrew. "These are great to do post-exercise.” Roughly 60 seconds of static stretching was associated with a reduced risk of injury and an increased range of motion, a 2016 study found—aiding in overall athletic performance.
  • Don't stretch your groin if...you’re recovering from an injury, especially in that area. "Consult your doctor or physical therapist before doing any concentrated movements,” Mulgrew says.

The 13 Best Groin Stretches

Here, Mulgrew and Yu share their groin stretch recommendations to do after a workout. Hold each stretch for 10 deep breaths or follow reps as indicated in each move.

1. Frog Squat

How to:

  1. Stand facing forward with feet slightly wider than hip-width apart, toes pointed outward.
  2. With arms stretching straight to the floor in front of you, sink into a squat. As you’re lowering, use your arms to gently press inner thighs outward.
  3. While your aim should be to touch the ground, ensure your heels stay flat on the floor. (If you can’t quite reach the floor, that’s okay!).

2. Sky Squat Reach

How to:

  1. Moving directly from the first stretch starting in the low squat, place left hand on the floor, continuing to gently push inner thigh outward.
  2. Reach right hand directly up to the ceiling, fingers pointed upward.
  3. With every breath, twist torso slightly further, reaching as high as you can. Left heel should raise slightly.
  4. Hold, then switch sides.

3. Wide-Stance Sumo Squat

How to:

  1. Start standing with feet wider than hips width and toes pointed out.
  2. Place either hand on top of your knee as you sink into a squat, thighs parallel to the floor.
  3. Inhale deeply as you twist your right shoulder downwards.
  4. Continue to inhale and exhale, and with each breath, attempt to twist your torso further.
  5. Hold, then switch sides.

4. Prying Goblet

How to:

  1. Start in a wide-squat stance position and sit butt as low as your can.
  2. While keeping feet flat on the ground, bend both elbows to place both hands together in front of you at chest level.
  3. Press knees gently apart with elbows.
  4. Shift weight toward right foot as you rotate body slightly toward the right.
  5. Hold position for one second and repeat to the opposite side. Do 10-12 reps on each side. With each repetition, try to go a bit deeper into the range.

Pro tip: You can start with bodyweight and progress to holding a kettlebell at chest level for added challenge.

5. Wide-Leg Forward Fold

How to:

  1. Start sitting on the mat with both legs straight out in front of you.
  2. Widen legs until you feel a mild stretch at the inner thigh area.
  3. While keeping back flat, slowly hinge forward from the hips and reach forward with arms.
  4. Place hands down on the mat in front of you.
  5. Reach as far as is comfortable, attempting to reach further with each exhale for 30 seconds.

Pro tip: You can adjust the depth of the stretch by closing the width of the legs or by varying the depth of your forward reach.

6. Wide-Leg Side Bend

How to:

  1. Start in a wide seated position on the mat, legs extended outwards.
  2. Move the right foot inward, pressing the bottom of the foot to the inner portion of the left thigh.
  3. Bend the torso to the left as you reach the left hand to the left knee, calf, or foot (whichever location is most comfortable).
  4. At the same time, either extend right arm upwards, fingers pointed toward the ceiling, or bend the right elbow, reaching the right hand behind the head.
  5. Hold, then switch sides.

7. Runner’s Lunge

How to:

  1. Get on all fours, facing the front of your mat.
  2. Plant fingertips or palms firmly into the ground as you extend left leg behind you, keeping knee rested or lifted slightly.
  3. Press left heel toward the back of the room.
  4. Bring right foot forward so it’s in line with right hand. Keep head lifted.
  5. Inhale and exhale, driving hips further into the ground with each breath.
  6. Hold, then switch sides.

8. Butterfly

How to:

  1. Move to a seated position and bring the bottoms of both feet together, with heels as close or far away from your groin as is comfortable.
  2. Bring hands to the outsides of either feet to stabilize you as you breathe in and out, gently allowing knees to drop with each breath.
  3. Option to lean forward with a flat back to deepen the stretch.

9. Alternating Lateral Lunge

How to:

  1. Start standing with feet about hips width apart.
  2. Widen your stance until you feel a mild stretch at the inner thigh with toes pointed forward or slightly outwards.
  3. Sit your hips back towards the right and allow your trunk to fold forward as you descend into a right lateral lunge.
  4. Push off the right heel, while maintaining your heel and forefoot in contact with the ground to return back to the center position.
  5. Sit the hips back towards the left and allow your trunk to fold forward as you descend into a left lateral lunge.
  6. Repeat for 10-12 reps.

Pro Tip: Keep the core engaged throughout the motion and keep a flat back throughout the exercise, says Yu.

10. Half Kneeling Adductor Rock

How to:

  1. Start in a half kneeling position with your left leg placed in front of you with the knee bent. Your right knee should be resting on the ground either directly beneath your torso, or slightly behind you, and bent at 90 degrees.
  2. Step your left foot out towards the left and place your hands on your hips.
  3. Shift your hips towards the left and your weight onto your left foot.
  4. Hold this position for 1-2 seconds before returning to center.
  5. Repeat for 8-12 reps on this side. With each repetition, try to shift the hips a bit deeper into the range.
  6. Return to the starting position and repeat to the opposite side.

11. Frogger Rockbacks

How to:

  1. Start on all fours in the quadruped position with both hands stacked under the shoulders and knees stacked under the hips.
  2. Spread knees apart until you feel a mild stretch to inner thigh and groin area.
  3. Turn the feet outwards and rest the front of shins down onto the mat.
  4. Push away from the mat with hands and sit the hips back towards heels.
  5. Hold this position for 1 second before returning back to a forward tabletop position.
  6. Repeat for 10-12 times. With each repetition, try to sit the hips a bit deeper.

Pro tip: If you are not able to rotate feet outwards to achieve an internally rotated hip position, you can tuck your toes under instead.

12. Lateral Lunge With Thoracic Side Bend

How to:

  1. Start in a tall kneeling position.
  2. Step left foot out to the left by placing your heel down on the mat and tuck right toes.
  3. Reach right arm overhead and towards the left and gently bend the trunk towards the left until you feel a stretch along the right side.
  4. Hold this position for 1-2 seconds before returning back to the center position.
  5. Complete 8-10 reps on this side. With each repetition, try to shift the hips a bit deeper into the range
  6. Return to the tall kneeling position and repeat to the opposite side.

  1. Stand with feet about hips-width apart with a chair (or other elevated surface) to your left.
  2. Lift left leg and place heel and lower leg onto the chair.
  3. With hands on hips, slowly bend sideways towards the left to feel a stretch at the inner thigh and groin area.
  4. Keep back flat and chest facing forward as you hold this position for 30 seconds.
  5. Stand up and turn so the chair is on your right.
